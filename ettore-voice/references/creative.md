# Creative register — fiction, travel, narrative essay

The storytelling voice, in Portuguese (extendable to English). Built from *Projeto Solitária* (fiction,
2011), *Histórias de Viagem* (travel, 2010), and *Engenharia das Letras* (narrative etymology essays,
2015). This is the one register where **play is maximal** and where narrative *architecture* — not just
sentence style — is the point. The cross-register invariants still hold (human in the frame, high
length variance, excavation), but the toolbox differs; some things banned in argument are legitimate
here.

## Sentence and beat rhythm

Fiction runs **short and cinematic**: median ~10 words, ~40% of sentences ≤8 words, high variance. The
move is a long establishing sentence that sets a scene, then a cascade of short fragments as emotional
beats:

> *Todos ansiavam a noite. / Todos exceto ele. / Ele, que todas as mulheres desejavam. Ele, que era bem
> vestido… Decidido inclusive, a não sair nesta sexta. / Ele, Razo.*

- **Fragments as beats.** One-line fragments carry the emotional turn: *"Ele, Razo." "A dor. Ah, a
  maldita dor." "Íntima."* Use them to land a moment, not to argue.
- **Anaphora is allowed here** (and only here). It's a rhythmic beat, not the analytical anti-pattern:
  *"Todos pensavam… Todos ansiavam… Todos exceto ele."* / *"Escrevia, porque lhe convinha. Escrevia,
  porque lhe era necessário…"* Build a small pressure with repetition, then break it.
- **Enumeration for suspense.** Counting slows time at a tense beat: *"Uma vez, duas vezes, três vezes,
  quatro vezes, cinco vezes, seis vezes. Atendeu."*

## Paragraph construction (the fractal signature)

The same long-accumulation-then-short-verdict rhythm that governs his sentences governs his
**paragraphs**. A chapter opens with one long, dense establishing paragraph (up to ~250 words: light,
place, sound, the social world), then breaks into a run of very short paragraphs to accelerate and to
isolate the protagonist:

> [long paragraph: the classroom, the bell, Friday-night party culture] → *Todos ansiavam a noite.* →
> *Todos exceto ele.* → *Ele, Razo.*

Rule of thumb: **establish long, then cut short**. The paragraph that isolates the character or lands
the beat should be a single line. Don't keep every paragraph the same size — the size *change* is the
device.

## Scene construction

- **Open on an establishing shot with one governing sense detail**, then pull to the character:
  *"Os derradeiros raios de sol entravam pelos vidros embaçados da janela da sala de aula…"* — light and
  fog first, people second.
- **Character-through-objects.** Introduce a character via their space: Daniela through the room she's
  proud of and chose the colors for; Marcela through the desk in the shadows where she writes. Interior
  by way of exterior.
- **Third-person limited, one consciousness per scene.** Stay inside one head at a time.
- **Dialogue in the Portuguese em-dash convention** (*– Vem. / – Foi.*), terse, doing work — a line of
  dialogue often carries the chapter's hook.

## Chapter and whole-story architecture

*Projeto Solitária* is planned, not improvised — worth imitating:

- **Rotating-POV structure.** Each chapter takes a different character (Razo → Daniela → Marcela), each
  a *solitária* — a solitary one, the title's promise. The book's unit is the person, not the plot beat.
- **Thematic plant up front.** Chapter one seeds the theme in passing dialogue — a professor quoting
  Plato, *"o amor é o desejo pelo algo que não se possui"* (love is desire for what one lacks) — and the
  whole story of lonely people pays it off. Plant the key early and quietly; never gloss it.
- **Close every chapter on a hook, never a resolution.** The hooks observed: a **name reveal** (*"meu
  nome é Catarina."*), a **suspended action** (the phone answered after six rings, chapter ends), a
  **turn inward** to pain (*"A dor. Ah, a maldita dor… Íntima."*). End on the door opening, not the room
  explained.

For **travel / memoir** (*Histórias de Viagem*): chronological arc built on a **motif that structures
repetition** — "a história se repetiu" (Boston loss → Hartford, same script, different ending). Escalate
to the climax with short sentences (*"Só mais alguns instantes." "Vencemos!"*), then close on one
reflective line (*"a viagem da minha vida"*). The motif is the spine; the reflection is the landing.

## Diachronic caution (what to imitate, what not)

His 2010 travel writing (age ~19) is the **least "him"**: earnest, formal, purple, uniform sentence
length, no reader address, no play (*"As longas e doloridas horas de vôo não aplacaram nossa
ansiedade"*). That youthful inflation is the *before*. Write to his **2011–2015** creative voice
instead — the one with rhythm, fragments, register spikes, and planted structure. When in doubt, cut a
flourish and shorten a sentence.

## Register spikes in creative essay mode

In narrative essays (*Engenharia das Letras*) the fiction rhythm fuses with the blog voice: **high/low
collision** (Proto-Indo-European roots a comma away from *"o pica das galáxias"* and *merda*), direct
reader address (*tu*: *"Viu as relações acontecendo?"*), the question→one-word-answer beat
(*"…call veio daí também!". / Não.*), staged mini-scenes, and open, companionable landings. Gaúcho
throughout (*bá, tri, tu*). See `blog.md` — the creative essay is the blog voice with more scene and
more play.

## Anti-patterns for creative work

- Youthful inflation and purple abstraction (the 2010 failure mode).
- Head-hopping within a scene; explaining a character instead of showing them in their space.
- Resolving a chapter instead of hooking it.
- Uniform paragraph length — losing the establish-long-then-cut-short device.
- A sealed aphoristic close. Even in fiction he lands on an image or a turn that opens, not a button.
