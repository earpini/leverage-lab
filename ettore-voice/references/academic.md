# Academic register — the reader-companion thesis

Peer-facing Ettore: papers, theses, abstracts, methods/results/discussion, research reports, reviewer
replies. Reference text: the TU Delft MSc thesis (*Urban Commons as a Driver of Inclusion*, 2022),
written to its **best** moments. One-line brief: **structured warmth** — rigorous scaffolding outside, a
human clearly present inside. The defining quality is that it *communicates*: it takes a
non-specialist reader through a complex argument without ever losing them or talking down.

## How he takes the reader along (the core of this register)

This is the thing to reproduce above all else. Six devices, all real in the thesis:

1. **State the accessibility promise, then keep it.** The foreword says the work is "described… in such
   a way that researchers, practitioners, and policy-makers of diverse backgrounds will find it
   accessible and useful" — and the body honors it. Write every technical passage as if a smart
   policymaker with no glossary is reading, because one is.
2. **Thread the research questions, visibly.** Questions get IDs (SQ1, SQ2, SQ3) and **every results
   section restates the question it answers**: "This section aims to answer SQ1, 'how are the locations
   of Urban Commons related to social and spatial factors in Amsterdam?'." The reader always knows which
   question they're inside. Never start analysis without naming its question.
3. **Give the reader a governing metaphor as a shared map — and extend it in the open.** The whole
   argument is navigated by one image: the *material wall*, the *immaterial wall*, and the *privilege
   wall* of access, plus *access streams*. He introduces it once, then uses it as plain terminology
   (18×), and when a finding exceeds the frame he **builds the frame further in front of the reader**:
   "in addition to the initially proposed material and immaterial walls, an ecosystem barrier to access."
   The reader watches the scaffold grow. Pick one governing image per document and let the section
   structure inherit it.
4. **Open chapters and sections with a one-paragraph map** that says what this part does ("this section
   describes the methods used to estimate…"). Signpost the *job* of the section before doing it.
5. **Anchor with forward/back references.** "as shown in Table…", "as seen in…" — light hands on the
   reader's shoulder so they never wonder where a claim came from.
6. **Warm structural titles as guide-posts.** "Looking Back: Limitations", "Looking Ahead: Future Work",
   "Socio-Academic Reflections". The titles have a pulse and signal a human guide; the prose under them
   stays formal.

The effect is a reader who is *escorted*, not lectured: told where they're going, given a map they can
hold, shown each finding pinned to a number or a name, and checked on at every turn.

## The voice to reproduce

- **Findings as countable facts, up front.** "Nine in ten residents of Amsterdam can reach a commons
  initiative by bike within 15 minutes." Plain subject-verb, no throat-clearing. Prefer this to "the
  analysis revealed a high degree of accessibility."
- **Credibility through named specifics.** Authors with years, methods by name (K-Means, Lloyd 1982,
  Scikit-Learn), laws in the original with a free translation. Never "studies show."
- **Qualitative evidence handled like journalism.** Interviewees keep their real quoted speech and
  hesitations; a field anecdote carries argumentative weight (the commoner who took a law degree to
  write her thesis on the legislation blocking her building purchase is *evidence*, not color). One
  vivid true anecdote per major claim beats a paragraph of abstraction.
- **Sentence rhythm with real variance.** Short declaratives ("Cities are fountains of innovation.")
  set up longer analytical sentences. Aim ~20–24 words average with wide spread.
- **A human in the frame where convention allows.** Forewords and reflections openly warm (family, the
  Akitas, "Enjoy the read!"); in the body, warmth shows through evidence choice and titles.

## The tics to auto-correct (documented from the thesis)

1. **Conjunctive-adverb chains** — *however* 49×, *therefore* 35×, *indeed* 29× (delete on sight),
   *moreover* 23×, *furthermore* 22×. One sentence in ~8 opened on a connector. Budget one per paragraph.
2. **Sentence pile-ups** — 8% of sentences run >40 words (max 278). Past ~35 words, split.
3. **Agentless passive outside Methods** (~9/1k). In Discussion/Conclusions, give each finding a concrete
   subject. Hunt "it was found that," "it can be inferred that."
4. **Stacked hedges** — one per claim; hedge the inference, not the measurement.
5. **Wordy connectors** — "in terms of" (17×), "in order to" (10×), "the fact that" (9×) → recast.
6. **Third-language residue** — article/agreement slips ("one of the most relevant result*s*"), "are not
   being able to" → "cannot." Extra look at these.
7. **Consistency drift** — one ordinal system, one hyphenation per term, names spelled once and reused.

## Language

English and Portuguese, same formal precise register. No gaúcho. In Portuguese, ABNT conventions unless
the venue says otherwise. **American spelling in English.**

## The bar

Would a policymaker with no glossary follow it, and would a reviewer find every claim pinned to a
number, a name, or a quote? Accessible *and* pinned down — that double test is the register.
