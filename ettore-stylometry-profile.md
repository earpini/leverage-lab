# Ettore Arpini — stylometry profile

*What makes your writing recognizably yours, across registers and languages.*

This is a working reference, not a verdict. It rests on three sources: (1) a quantitative pass over
your MSc thesis (*Urban Commons as a Driver of Inclusion*, TU Delft, 2022 — ~37,000 words of running
prose, cleaned of figures and tables); (2) the four voice profiles already distilled from a broader
corpus (academic, blog, essay, application); and (3) the three creative Portuguese docs — an
etymology-essay collection (2015), a work of fiction (2011), and a travel memoir (2010) — measured in
§8, which also gives a diachronic read from 2010 to 2022.

The numbers below are from the thesis corpus unless stated otherwise. They agree closely with the
counts recorded independently in the earlier academic profile (*however* 49 vs. 44, *therefore* 35 vs.
32, one sentence in ~8 opening on a connector vs. "one in seven") — the small gaps are cleaning
differences, not disagreement. Treat the thesis as one genre sampled deeply; the cross-register claims
lean on the profiles.

## 1. The invariant — what survives genre change

Strip away the register and eight things stay. These are the signature; everything in §4 onward is
detail underneath them.

1. **Excavation, not assertion.** The engine is curiosity, not thesis. You start from a small concrete
   itch — a word, a number, a scene — and *trace it to its root*: the origin, the actual mechanism, how
   the thing came to be. You dig; you don't announce a conclusion and defend it. This is the single most
   load-bearing trait, and it's why thesis-led contrarian openers read as not-you. (A note on etymology:
   it shows up heavily in *Engenharia das Letras* because that book is literally *about* word origins —
   it's the subject, not a personal stylistic tic. The invariant is the excavation *move*; etymology was
   just what you happened to be excavating that time. In the thesis and the essays you excavate policy
   mechanisms and definitions with the same move and no etymology in sight.)

2. **Named specifics as the unit of credibility.** The through-line of *every* register. Authors with
   years, laws in the original language with a translation, hard numbers with a source, real proper
   nouns over categories. Never "studies show," never "various factors." In the thesis, content words
   are almost all concrete nouns (*commons, Amsterdam, respondents, neighbourhood*); the credibility is
   in the density of named things, not in adjectives.

3. **A concrete image promoted to load-bearing vocabulary.** Your most distinctive structural move:
   take one image and make it the *terminology*, used consistently through the analysis — the
   *material wall* (101×), the *immaterial wall* (98×), *access streams* (35×), *policy confluence*
   (9×), the city as *ecosystem* (63×). You don't decorate with metaphor; you *build* with one governing
   image and let the section structure inherit it.

4. **Wide sentence-length variance — long accumulation, short verdict.** Mean 21.7 words, but standard
   deviation 16.2 (a spread of ~0.75× the mean, very high). 18% of sentences run ≤8 words; 13% run
   >35. The rhythm is a stacked cumulative sentence that earns a conclusion, then a short declarative
   that lands it. The shortest sentence in a paragraph tends to be the most important one.

5. **Framework brain, announced.** You think in named structures and you show the scaffolding: research
   questions get IDs (SQ1, SQ2…), components get bolded labels, architectures get numbered (1… 2… 3…).
   You don't hide the bones. Concepts arrive as named pairs ("*Coordination power. Persuasion power.*").

6. **Hedge, then commit.** Genuine epistemic hedging runs through the analysis — *could* is your
   workhorse (66×, far ahead of *may*/*might*) — but a passage never *ends* on a hedge. The honesty is
   in the qualifier; the commitment always arrives.

7. **A human visibly in the frame.** Even in a formal thesis, first-person *I* appears 76× and the
   foreword thanks two Akitas by name (Kita and Thor) and signs off "Enjoy the read!". Warmth shows
   through evidence choice, warm section titles ("Looking Back: Limitations"), and parenthetical asides
   — not through chattiness.

8. **Parenthetical thinking.** You think in qualifications and asides: 637 opening parentheses, 17.5
   per 1,000 words — unusually high. In the essay register the parentheses carry the jokes; in the
   academic register they carry the caveats. Either way, the aside is native to you.

**The landing test.** Your pieces land *open* and often *rueful* — a turn, a question handed to the
reader, a wry admission ("we have a gift for owning the right asset at the wrong angle"). They do **not**
snap shut on an engineered aphorism. That sealed-aphorism close is a real style — it's the Coimbra
crônica register — but it is the opposite of yours, and it's the fastest way to tell a fake from you.

## 3. Register map — same DNA, different dial settings

| | **Academic** (thesis/paper) | **Essay** (policy/op-ed) | **Blog** (personal) | **Application** (letter/SoP) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Led by | question, threaded (SQ1…) | framework, named | **itch**, chased | concrete hook → relevance |
| Opening | one-paragraph map | story / plain fact / question | the itch, never a thesis | real event + named source + number |
| Register mix | formal, even | **hard switches**: Latinate ↔ colloquial drop | warm, playful; full gaúcho in casual PT | sincere, a little aspirational |
| Signature move | metaphor→terminology | question → fragment answer ("Well, leverage.") | trace-to-root, cross-lingual | trajectory in named specifics |
| Voice-in-frame | titles, forewords, *I* | ventriloquised actors speak | talks straight to you | first person throughout |
| Landing | finding, pinned to a number | short verdict, hands off the baton | wry / a turn, **open** | forward vision, plain |
| Portuguese | standard academic (ABNT) | n/a (fluent EN only) | **full gaúcho** (*Bá, Capaz, tu, tô*) | standard formal |

**The constants across all four columns:** named specifics as credibility; a governing framework or
image; high sentence-length variance; hedge-then-commit; a human in the frame. The *dial* that moves
most is play and register-mixing (max in blog, zero in application) and Portuguese register (full
gaúcho only in casual posts).

## 4. Positive markers — the tells that it's *you*

- **Trace-to-root openings.** "I went looking for what 'sovereignty' actually means last week, and it
  threw me…" — the itch, then the excavation. The *move* is digging to the origin/mechanism; the object
  can be a word, a policy, a number, or a definition. (When the subject happens to be language itself —
  as in the etymology essays — the digging *is* etymology, but that's the topic doing the work, not a
  stylistic ornament.)
- **Rhetorical question → one-word / fragment answer.** "Não." "Arrá!" "Well, leverage." "This one is
  simple." Transitions happen through a question; the strongest answer is a fragment.
- **Ventriloquised actors.** You give a party invented direct speech rather than describing its
  position — traders who "could literally say 'I won't buy any soy that I can't verify…'". At least
  once per major section, someone speaks.
- **The governing metaphor as vocabulary** (walls, streams, confluence, ecosystem) — introduced once,
  then used as plain terminology, capitalised into the analysis.
- **Numbers with an attribution instinct.** You leave `[source]` markers rather than float an uncited
  figure, and you flag soft numbers ("roughly 88%").
- **Parentheses that carry wit or caveat** — "(yes, months, not years)", "(notice that Google appears
  twice)". If a parenthesis only defines a term, it's probably not doing your kind of work.
- **Warm structural titles** over neutral ones: "Looking Back: Limitations", "Looking Ahead".
- **Rueful, specific landings.** "we have a gift for owning the right asset at the wrong angle."

## 5. Known tics — the residue to correct (all real, from the thesis)

These are habits, not identity. The academic profile already lists the fixes; the counts here say how
hard each one bites.

1. **Conjunctive-adverb chains.** One sentence in ~8 opens on a connector; *indeed* alone appears 29×
   (delete on sight). Budget: one per paragraph, only on a real turn.
2. **Sentence pile-ups.** 8% of sentences exceed 40 words (max observed: 278). Almost any sentence past
   ~35 words is two sentences — split it.
3. **Agentless passive outside Methods** (~9/1k). Fine in Methods; in Discussion, give every finding a
   concrete subject (the data, the respondents, this study, I). Hunt "it was found that."
4. **Stacked hedges.** One hedge per claim. Hedge the inference, not the measurement.
5. **Wordy connectors.** *in terms of* 17×, *in order to* 10×, *the fact that* 9× → recast or shorten.
6. **Third-language residue.** Article/agreement slips ("one of the most relevant result*s*"), and the
   tell-tale "are not being able to" (→ "cannot"). Give these an extra look. In the essay register the
   equivalent is PT calques to fix silently: *mediatic* → "made for the news"; *sensitize about* → "get
   people to care about"; *on the field* → "in the field".
7. **Consistency drift.** One ordinal system, one hyphenation per term, names spelled once and reused.

## 6. The anti-signature — what reads as *not you* (or as AI)

Consistent across all four profiles. If a draft does these, it isn't yours:

- **Thesis-led contrarian opener.** A packaged claim up front instead of a chased one. "Here's the part
  nobody is saying out loud."
- **The antithesis button.** "The question isn't whether X, it's whether Y."
- **Reveal / insistence framing.** "here's what gets left out of the story," "the fine print people
  skip," "the part I want to insist on." You are a companion on a thought journey, not a debunker with a
  secret.
- **Vague authority.** "studies show," "various factors," "experts agree."
- **Grand abstraction / moralising closer.** "the global challenges of our era," "transformative,"
  "ever-evolving landscape," and the closer that restates the point as a lesson.
- **Aphoristic button ending / sealed close.** "A redline you cannot verify is a press release." One per
  essay maximum, and the *habitual* sealed close belongs to a different writer entirely (Coimbra), not
  you.
- **Anaphora triads.** "Not a scandal. Not a scientific reversal. Not the Amazon burning." Punchy, but
  you build by accumulation, not repetition.
- **Em-dash chains as the default connective.** Your dashes are genuine but occasional; you connect with
  commas + discourse markers. (This is the one place the de-ai pass *under*-counts you — keep your
  dashes, just don't let them become the only joint.)
- **Flat, even register.** No spikes, no gear-shifts, uniform medium sentences. The absence of variance
  is itself a tell.

## 7. The cross-lingual layer

- **Two languages, same person.** English and Portuguese both carry the full signature. The academic and
  application registers stay standard in both.
- **Gaúcho only in casual PT.** *Bá, Capaz, tu, tô, arrá* — the regional lexicon is exclusive to
  personal/blog Portuguese. Never in English, never in formal writing.
- **The excavation move is language-agnostic.** In *Engenharia das Letras* (the etymology essays) the
  object being excavated is words, so the digging takes the form of chasing a root to PIE, staging a
  little scene, running the cross-lingual chain (schijten → scheissen → excremento), and landing on a
  kinship reveal. Swap the object and the *move* is identical: "sovereignty from *super*" in English, a
  policy mechanism in the thesis. Etymology is one thing you've excavated, not the excavating itself.
- **Byline:** Ettore Arpini. Never "de Lacerda". **American spelling throughout** (analyze, color,
  behavior, -ize) — this holds in prose and code alike; do not "Britishize" his writing.

## 8. Creative register (Portuguese, 2010–2015)

Three docs, three modes, and — usefully — three points in time before the 2022 thesis. Numbers below
are from the PDF extracts.

| Doc | Year | Mode | Words | Sent. mean / std | ≤8w | Tell |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Histórias de Viagem 3 | 2010 (~age 19) | travel memoir | 650 | 12.4 / 7.8 | 35% | earnest, no play |
| Projeto Solitária | 2011 | fiction | 3,150 | 13.7 / 11.1 | 40% | cinematic fragments |
| Engenharia das Letras | 2015 | etymology essays | 3,150 | 19.0 / 14.8 | 25% | **the voice, formed** |

**Fiction mode (Projeto Solitária).** Short, cinematic sentences — median 10 words, 40% of them ≤8 —
built for pace. Scene-first openings ("The last rays of sun came through the fogged classroom
windows…"), then dramatic one-line fragments as beats: *"Todos exceto ele." "Ele, Razo." "Não, não,
hoje cumpriria seu compromisso."* Notably, fiction is where you *do* use **anaphora** ("Todos
pensavam… Todos ansiavam… Todos exceto ele. Ele, que… Ele, que…") — the very device the essay profile
lists as an anti-signature for *analytical* writing. So anaphora isn't off-limits for you; it's a
storytelling tool you keep out of argument. Same person, different toolbox.

**Essay mode (Engenharia das Letras, 2015).** This is where the recognizable voice is fully assembled,
in Portuguese, at ~24: high sentence-length variance (std 14.8), the rhetorical-question→one-word-answer
tic in its native habitat (*"Lendo isso a primeira coisa que tu pode pensar é 'bá, call veio daí
também!'." / "Não."*), direct reader address by name of the second person (*tu* 33×, *"Viu as relações
acontecendo?"*, *"não te preocupa"*), and the **high/low register collision** that's a core signature —
Proto-Indo-European roots and Roman law sitting a comma away from *"largar um barro,"* *"o pica das
galáxias,"* and *merda*. Full gaúcho throughout (*bá, tri, tu*). The pieces even *land* the way your
English essays do — open and companionable, not sealed: *"na verdade, shit e science são primos e
vieram do mesmo lugar."*

**Travel mode (Histórias de Viagem, 2010).** The earliest and least "you" — earnest, literary, formal,
first-person-plural, no jokes, no reader address, no gaúcho, and the lowest sentence variance in the
whole corpus (std 7.8). Purple young-writer flourishes (*"As longas e doloridas horas de vôo não
aplacaram nossa ansiedade,"* *"Vitória!!!"*, *"a viagem da minha vida"*). This is exactly the *youthful
inflation* the application profile says to trim — captured here at the source.

### The diachronic arc (2010 → 2022)

- **2010 (travel, 19):** earnest and inflated, uniform sentences, no play, no reader in the room.
- **2011 (fiction, 20):** rhythm arrives — high variance, cinematic fragments, scene-led, anaphora as a
  storytelling beat.
- **2015 (essays, 24):** the voice crystallizes — register collision, reader address (*tu*), the
  question→fragment tic, excavation, open landings. Recognizably the writer of the 2020s essays.
- **2022 (thesis, 27):** same DNA — variance, named specifics, a governing image, warmth in the frame —
  in a formal register with the play deliberately stripped and the discipline gained.

**Stable the whole way:** a human visibly in the frame (first person everywhere), concrete
scene-staging, and — from 2011 on — wide sentence-length variance. **What matured:** the register
collision and reader address (present by 2015, absent in 2010), and the trade of youthful inflation for
control. The engine was always there; you learned to modulate it and to know which register gets which
tools.

## 9. Using this profile

- **Drafting as you:** pick the register (§3), lead with the right opening, keep named specifics dense,
  choose one governing image, vary sentence length hard, hedge-then-commit, land open. Then run the §5
  tic-pass and the §6 anti-signature check.
- **Auditing a draft:** the fastest four tests — (a) does it *open* on an itch or a packaged thesis?
  (b) is the credibility in named things or in adjectives? (c) is there real sentence-length variance?
  (d) does it land open, or snap shut on an aphorism? Two wrong answers and it isn't you yet.
- **Feeding the voice skills:** this is consistent with the four `ettore-*-voice` skills and can be used
  to tune them; §2's numbers are the empirical anchor they were missing.
